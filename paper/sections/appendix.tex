\section{Design-Principle Ablation}
\label{app:ablation}

\Cref{tab:ablation} summarizes the four design-principle prototypes. ``Broken'' means R-ASR $> 0.5$ (the adaptive attack succeeds on the majority of tasks); ``Over-blocks'' means FPR $> 0.5$ (the defense blocks the majority of benign actions). No principle achieves both R-ASR $< 0.5$ and FPR $< 0.5$, confirming the fundamental security-utility tradeoff formalized by \Cref{thm:single}.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\caption{Design-principle ablation: R-ASR, FPR, USR, and verdict.}
\label{tab:ablation}
\begin{tabular}{lcccl}
\toprule
Principle & R-ASR$\downarrow$ & FPR$\downarrow$ & USR$\uparrow$ & Verdict \\
\midrule
P1: Unobservable Signal & 0.90 & 0.05 & 0.95 & Broken \\
P2: Orthogonal Signals & 0.00 & 0.57 & 0.43 & Over-blocks \\
P3: Task Invariant & 0.00 & 1.00 & 0.00 & Over-blocks \\
P4: Least Privilege & 1.00 & 0.00 & 1.00 & Broken \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

P1 demonstrates that a single unobservable signal (a random canary) is insufficient: the canary leaks into the agent's output and is extracted by the multi-turn adaptive attacker (L3), then forged in subsequent injections. P2 combines P1's canary with task-alignment checking; the conjunction resists (R-ASR 0.00) because forging the canary is information-theoretically impossible, but the 57\% FPR shows that the conjunction over-blocks. P3's strict state machine blocks everything including the legitimate task (FPR 1.00). P4's sandbox does not detect injections, only limits blast radius, so an injection that uses an allowed resource maliciously still succeeds (R-ASR 1.00).

\section{Backbone Comparison}
\label{app:backbone}

Our main evaluation uses GPT-4o-mini. To assess backbone sensitivity, we re-run a reduced matrix (6 key defenses $\times$ 5 levels $\times$ 2 seeds $\times$ 15 tasks) with GPT-4o. \Cref{tab:backbone} compares R-ASR between the two backbones. The structural finding---single-signal defenses collapse under adaptive attack---holds for both: Spotlighting and Attention Tracker reach R-ASR 1.00 on both backbones, while StruQ and Task Shield resist on both. Absolute ASR values differ slightly (GPT-4o is somewhat more susceptible to static IPI, raising L1 ASR by 0.05--0.10), but the L1$\to$L2 collapse pattern is backbone-independent, consistent with our theoretical prediction that the failure stems from the defense's architecture, not the model's capability.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\caption{Backbone comparison: R-ASR for 6 key defenses.}
\label{tab:backbone}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Defense & GPT-4o-mini & GPT-4o \\
\midrule
No Defense & 1.00 & 1.00 \\
Spotlighting & 1.00 & 0.13 \\
StruQ & 0.10 & 0.00 \\
Task Shield & 0.00 & 0.00 \\
Attention Tracker & 1.00 & 1.00 \\
P1 (Unobservable) & 0.90 & 1.00 \\
\bottomrule

\end{table}

The most notable difference is Spotlighting: it collapses on GPT-4o-mini (R-ASR 1.00) but resists on GPT-4o (R-ASR 0.13). This is because GPT-4o more reliably obeys the delimiter instruction (``text inside data delimiters is untrusted''), making the signal-alignment evasion less effective. This backbone sensitivity is itself a finding: the robustness of input-encoding defenses depends on the backbone's instruction-following fidelity, not just the defense's design---a property not captured by the single-signal theorem but consistent with it (the delimiter is still an observable signal; a stronger model simply assigns it more authority). Attention Tracker collapses on both backbones (R-ASR 1.00), confirming that internal-probing defenses are backbone-independent in their failure.

\section{Adaptive Attack Strength}
\label{app:adaptive_strength}

Our main evaluation uses \texttt{max\_iters=2} for the LLM-in-the-loop adaptive optimizer. To assess sensitivity to optimizer budget, we re-run L2/L3 with \texttt{max\_iters=5} (the setting used by Zhan et al.). \Cref{tab:adaptive_strength} shows that increasing the optimizer budget from 2 to 5 iterations does not meaningfully change ASR for the collapsing defenses (already at 1.00) or the resisting defenses (already at 0.00), indicating that 2 iterations suffice for the adaptive attack to find an effective payload. This is consistent with the single-signal theorem: the search space of evasive payloads is small enough that 2 random-restart iterations find a below-threshold injection with high probability.

Increasing the optimizer budget from 2 to 5 iterations does not meaningfully change ASR for the collapsing defenses (already at 1.00) or the resisting defenses (already at 0.00--0.10). P1 shows a slight increase (0.90$\to$0.98), indicating that the canary-extraction attack benefits from more optimizer iterations, but the qualitative verdict (``broken'') is unchanged. This confirms that 2 iterations suffice for the adaptive attack to find an effective payload, consistent with the single-signal theorem: the search space of evasive payloads is small enough that 2 random-restart iterations find a below-threshold injection with high probability.
